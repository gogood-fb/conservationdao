Implementation Strategy

A roadmap for integrating DAO structures into Re:Wild's conservation efforts

Strategic Approach

Implementing DAO structures at Re:Wild requires a thoughtful, phased approach that balances innovation with organizational stability. We recommend the following strategic framework:

Guiding Principles
  1. Conservation First: All DAO implementations must demonstrably advance Re:Wild's conservation mission
  2. Start Small, Scale Gradually: Begin with focused pilots before broader implementation
  3. Hybrid Approach: Combine traditional organizational structures with DAO elements
  4. Inclusive Design: Ensure accessibility for all stakeholders, regardless of technical expertise
  5. Adaptive Management: Build in mechanisms for continuous learning and evolution
  6. Regulatory Compliance: Maintain clear legal and regulatory compliance in all jurisdictions
  7. Technical Robustness: Prioritize security, reliability, and user experience

Implementation Roadmap

We recommend a four-phase implementation approach over a 24+ month period:

Phase 1: Pilot Implementation (0-6 months)
Key Activities:
  • Implement the Conservation Microfinance DAO pilot in one project area
  • Establish technical infrastructure and governance framework
  • Develop educational materials and onboarding processes
  • Create monitoring and evaluation framework
  • Build internal capacity through training and recruitment
Success Criteria:
  • Functional DAO with active community participation
  • At least 5 community conservation projects funded
  • Transparent governance and fund allocation
  • Positive user experience for all stakeholders
  • Clear documentation of lessons learned
Phase 2: Evaluation and Refinement (6-12 months)
Key Activities:
  • Conduct comprehensive evaluation of pilot outcomes
  • Refine technical infrastructure and governance based on lessons learned
  • Develop strategy for scaling or implementing additional pilots
  • Engage with broader conservation community to share learnings
  • Explore potential partnerships with other conservation organizations
Success Criteria:
  • Comprehensive evaluation report with clear insights
  • Refined DAO model with improved functionality
  • Clear strategy for next phase of implementation
  • Increased internal capacity and expertise
  • External recognition of innovative approach
Phase 3: Scaling and Integration (12-24 months)
Key Activities:
  • Expand successful pilot to additional project areas or implement new pilot types
  • Integrate DAO elements with existing Re:Wild systems and processes
  • Develop interoperability between different DAO implementations
  • Create more advanced governance and token economic models
  • Build strategic partnerships with technical and conservation organizations
Success Criteria:
  • Multiple functioning DAO implementations across different contexts
  • Seamless integration with Re:Wild's existing operations
  • Measurable improvements in conservation outcomes
  • Increased funding and community engagement
  • Established position as conservation DAO leader
Phase 4: Ecosystem Development (24+ months)
Key Activities:
  • Develop a comprehensive conservation DAO ecosystem
  • Create standards and templates for conservation DAOs
  • Establish cross-DAO collaboration mechanisms
  • Explore integration with broader Web3 ecosystem
  • Advocate for supportive regulatory frameworks
Success Criteria:
  • Functioning conservation DAO ecosystem with multiple participants
  • Significant increase in conservation funding through DAO mechanisms
  • Measurable impact on global conservation priorities
  • Recognition as global leader in conservation innovation
  • Sustainable, self-reinforcing system of conservation DAOs

Key Success Factors

Based on our analysis, the following factors will be critical for successful implementation:

  • Executive sponsorship: Clear support and engagement from Re:Wild leadership
  • Dedicated resources: Sufficient funding and staffing for implementation
  • Technical expertise: Access to high-quality blockchain development capabilities
  • Community engagement: Meaningful participation from local communities and stakeholders
  • Adaptive approach: Willingness to learn, iterate, and adjust based on outcomes
  • Patience: Recognition that building effective DAO structures takes time
  • Balance: Maintaining focus on conservation outcomes while exploring technological innovation
  • Knowledge sharing: Open approach to sharing learnings with the broader conservation community

Organizational Requirements

Successfully implementing DAO structures will require several organizational adaptations:

Team Structure

We recommend establishing a dedicated DAO Implementation Team with the following roles:

  • DAO Program Director (oversight and strategy)
  • Technical Lead (blockchain and smart contract expertise)
  • Community Engagement Specialist
  • Conservation Science Advisor
  • Legal/Compliance Specialist
  • UX/UI Designer

This team should report directly to senior leadership and work closely with existing conservation teams.

Partnerships

Key partnerships to develop include:

  • Technical partners (blockchain development firms)
  • Legal advisors with DAO/crypto expertise
  • Academic institutions for research support
  • Other conservation organizations for knowledge sharing
  • Web3 communities and foundations for funding and expertise
  • Local community organizations in implementation areas
Capacity Building

Investment in the following capacity areas is essential:

  • Technical training for staff on blockchain fundamentals
  • Governance design and facilitation skills
  • Community education and onboarding capabilities
  • Token economic modeling expertise
  • Impact measurement in decentralized contexts
  • Regulatory compliance knowledge
Funding Strategy

Potential funding sources include:

  • Dedicated innovation budget allocation
  • Web3 foundation grants
  • Impact investment for conservation technology
  • Corporate partnerships with blockchain companies
  • Traditional conservation funders interested in innovation
  • Revenue from successful DAO implementations

Risk Management

A proactive risk management approach should include:

Risk Category Mitigation Strategies
Regulatory
  • Engage legal counsel with blockchain expertise
  • Focus initial implementation in jurisdictions with clearer regulatory frameworks
  • Develop hybrid legal structures that bridge traditional and DAO models
  • Maintain ongoing regulatory monitoring and compliance
Technical
  • Conduct thorough security audits of all smart contracts
  • Implement multi-signature requirements for treasury management
  • Use established, well-tested blockchain platforms
  • Develop backup systems and contingency plans
  • Start with smaller-scale implementations before scaling
Governance
  • Design governance systems with appropriate checks and balances
  • Implement token distribution that prevents concentration of power
  • Create clear escalation paths for governance disputes
  • Maintain traditional governance backstops for critical decisions
  • Develop comprehensive governance documentation and training
Operational
  • Develop clear roles and responsibilities for all participants
  • Create detailed operational procedures and documentation
  • Implement phased funding releases based on milestone achievement
  • Establish regular monitoring and reporting mechanisms
  • Develop contingency plans for key personnel changes
Reputational
  • Develop clear communication strategy about DAO implementation
  • Focus messaging on conservation impact rather than technology
  • Engage stakeholders early in the design process
  • Maintain transparency about both successes and challenges
  • Develop crisis communication plans for potential issues

Conclusion

Implementing DAO structures at Re:Wild represents a significant opportunity to pioneer new models of conservation governance, funding, and impact. By taking a thoughtful, phased approach that prioritizes conservation outcomes while embracing innovation, Re:Wild can position itself as a leader in this emerging space while delivering tangible benefits for biodiversity and communities.

The journey will require patience, adaptability, and a willingness to learn from both successes and failures. However, the potential rewards—more sustainable funding, increased stakeholder engagement, greater transparency, and ultimately more effective conservation—make this a worthwhile endeavor.
