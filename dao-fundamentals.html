DAO Fundamentals

Understanding the core concepts of Decentralized Autonomous Organizations for conservation

What is a DAO?

A Decentralized Autonomous Organization (DAO) is a community-led entity with no central authority. It is fully autonomous and transparent: smart contracts lay the foundational rules, execute the agreed upon decisions, and at any point, proposals, voting, and even the very code itself can be publicly audited.

DAOs are internet-native organizations collectively owned and managed by their members. They have built-in treasuries that are only accessible with the approval of their members. Decisions are made via proposals the group votes on during a specified period.

Key Characteristics of DAOs
Decentralization

No single entity has complete control; governance is distributed among members according to established rules.

Autonomy

Operations are automated through smart contracts, reducing the need for centralized management.

Transparency

All rules, transactions, and activities are recorded on a blockchain and visible to all participants.

Community Governance

Decision-making power is distributed among members, typically through voting mechanisms.

How DAOs Work

DAOs operate through a combination of blockchain technology, smart contracts, and governance mechanisms:

Technical Foundation
Blockchain

DAOs are built on blockchain platforms (like Ethereum, Polygon, or Solana) that provide the infrastructure for decentralized applications. The blockchain serves as an immutable ledger of all DAO activities and transactions.

Smart Contracts

Smart contracts are self-executing programs that automatically implement the rules and decisions of the DAO. They handle everything from membership management to treasury operations and voting processes.

Tokens

Most DAOs use tokens to represent membership, voting rights, or ownership shares. These tokens can be distributed based on contributions, purchased, or earned through participation.

Governance Process
  1. Proposal Submission: Members submit proposals for changes, actions, or funding allocations.
  2. Discussion Period: The community discusses and refines proposals before voting.
  3. Voting: Members vote on proposals, with voting power typically determined by token holdings or other contribution metrics.
  4. Execution: If approved, proposals are automatically executed through smart contracts or implemented by designated teams.
  5. Monitoring: Results and impacts are tracked transparently on the blockchain.

Web3 Tools for DAOs

Web3 refers to the next generation of internet applications built on decentralized blockchain technology. Several Web3 tools are particularly relevant for DAO operations:

Essential Web3 Tools
Governance Platforms
  • Snapshot: Off-chain voting system
  • Aragon: DAO creation and management
  • Colony: Reputation-based governance
  • Tally: On-chain governance dashboard
Treasury Management
  • Gnosis Safe: Multi-signature treasury
  • Parcel: Payroll and expenses
  • Llama: Treasury analytics
  • Superfluid: Streaming payments
Coordination Tools
  • Coordinape: Peer-to-peer rewards
  • SourceCred: Contribution tracking
  • Dework: Task management
  • Guild: Token-gated access
Funding Mechanisms
  • Gitcoin: Quadratic funding
  • Juicebox: Crowdfunding platform
  • Mirror: Writing and fundraising
  • Syndicate: Investment DAOs

Types of DAOs

DAOs come in many forms, each designed for specific purposes and communities:

Common DAO Categories
Protocol DAOs

Govern decentralized protocols and applications, making decisions about technical upgrades, parameter changes, and treasury allocations.

Examples: MakerDAO, Uniswap

Investment DAOs

Pool capital from members to invest in assets, projects, or other ventures, with returns distributed back to members.

Examples: MetaCartel, The LAO

Social DAOs

Focus on building communities around shared interests, with membership often represented by NFTs or tokens.

Examples: Friends With Benefits, Bankless DAO

Service DAOs

Provide services to other projects, such as development, design, or marketing, with revenue shared among members.

Examples: RaidGuild, LexDAO

Collector DAOs

Pool resources to acquire and manage collections of assets, particularly digital art and NFTs.

Examples: PleasrDAO, FlamingoDAO

Impact DAOs

Focus on creating positive social or environmental impact through collective action and funding.

Examples: KlimaDAO, Gitcoin

Conservation DAOs: Emerging Examples

Several DAOs have emerged specifically focused on environmental conservation and sustainability:

Carbonland DAO

Carbonland DAO aims to protect forests and their carbon sequestration capacity through collective ownership and management of forest land.

Key Features:

  • Tokenized land ownership through NFTs
  • Carbon credit generation and distribution
  • Decentralized land management decisions

KlimaDAO

KlimaDAO aims to accelerate the price appreciation of carbon assets by creating a liquid carbon-backed currency and ecosystem, driving climate action.

Key Features:

  • Carbon-backed treasury and currency
  • Automated market operations
  • Carbon retirement functionality
Visit Website
MesoReefDAO

MesoReefDAO focuses on regenerating coral reef conservation through decentralized science (DeSci) and Web3 tools.

Key Features:

  • Regenerative science framework
  • Decentralized research funding
  • Community-driven conservation projects
Visit Website
Big Green DAO

Big Green DAO is the first non-profit led philanthropic DAO, focused on food justice and sustainable gardening initiatives across the United States.

Key Features:

  • Decentralized grantmaking for food projects
  • Community governance of funding decisions
  • Transparent impact reporting
